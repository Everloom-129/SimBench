CALVIN
Chaining Atlas

CALVIN (Composing Actions from Language and Vision) is a tabletop benchmark whose signature is the long-horizon language chain: a policy must complete five language instructions in a row from raw RGB-D + proprioception, re-grounding a fresh natural-language command at each step. This atlas covers the 34 tasks and 389 crowd-sourced instructions, the four play environments A·B·C·D, and the official chaining leaderboard — where the real story is how fast success decays from task 1 to task 5, and how far it falls on an unseen environment.

How CALVIN scores. At eval, the agent is given a sequence of 5 instructions and must finish each before the next is revealed — if it fails one, the chain stops. The headline metric is average rollout length (mean tasks completed in a row, out of 5). The standard splits: D→D (single env), ABCD→D (train all, test D), and the hard ABC→D (train A,B,C — test the unseen environment D). Instructions are real human annotations, so each task carries many paraphrases (here place_in_slider has 20, the push_* tasks 9 each).

The same models that chain ~4–5 tasks on a seen environment can collapse on the unseen one: HULC falls from 3.06 to 0.67, and even GR-1 drops from 4.21 to 3.06. CALVIN's ABC→D split is precisely a visual-generalization stress test — reporting ABCD→D alone hides it.
